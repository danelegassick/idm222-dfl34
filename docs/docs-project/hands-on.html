Hands-On Design

While digital design takes up a majority of my portfolio, I want to highlight my love for building tangible products as well. Working entirely digitally can be exhausting, and leads to quick burnout. To counter this, I began working on physical projects like rug-making and table-making during my first co-op.

Hands-on

Building

My interest in tufting, a.k.a. rug making, sprouted after seeing a surge in creative rugs online. I noticed that people had bright and unique rugs on their floor, and I wanted to know how those were made. After a deep dive into tufting, I learned that all I needed were a few tools to get started. So, with my first co-op paycheck I got started.

TOOLS

My setup included a wooden frame with carpet tacks that I could clamp onto my desk, a cut-pile tufting gun, a hot glue gun, a projector, industrial carpet adhesive, clippers, and sheep shears.

GETTING STARTED

The steps to start tufting are pretty straightforward. Get your cloth taut on the frame, project your design (with a mirrored image), mark out where your colors will be, and start tufting.


TUFTING

Set up 2 lines of yarn to run through the tip of your tufting gun, set the speed to something comfortable, and press the gun through the cloth. Then pull the trigger and navigate your gun around to create outlines for each color. Then fill in your blocked out areas. The backside of your cloth will have that classic rug texture.

SEALING

Next step requires adhesive. The yarn has to be locked in and the bottom of the rug has to be somewhat presentable. In this step, you apply a thick layer of adhesive to the back of your rug, then seal that between another piece of fabric that will work as your underside. Then it has to rest for at least a day.

CARVING

Once this rug is all locked in, you want to cut it out of the frame. The edges of the tufting fabric will be folded under the rug and glued down. You should have a great looking rug now, but I always liked to refine my designs with shears and clippers. Shears ensure even distribution of yarn and clean up any stray pieces. Then clippers can be used along the edges of each color to make clean lines. Then give it a good vacuum and you are done!

A TABLE?!

Similarly to my interest in rug making, I also got interested in table-making through the internet. People had cool tables, and I wanted one too. So I made one. This took much less research. I had a clear plan in mind from the beginning.

DESIGN IN MIND

Mac Millerâ€™s mixtape, Faces, has one of my favorite covers of all time, and I knew it would make for a great table. I trimmed the edges to make a circular-ish shape that would give it a unique silhouette.

THE PROCESS

From Home Depot, I got a thin, circular piece of wood then projected the design onto it. I traced the design then went to get some help cutting it out. This was a complex cut that I am just not skilled enough to pull off, so I got some help from my grandpa. With this cut out, I sanded the edges and started painting.

TIMELINE

This had to be done in a 3 day sprint, so some of my family helped by taking turns painting in the large yellow spaces. After two coats of paint were applied, I drilled in holes for a lazy susan, and connected it to the wooden base and metal legs I got from Home Depot. This allowed for it to be spun around giving it a little extra flair.

EPOXY

Last, an epoxy coating was applied over the top to create a glossy and professional finish to the table. As my family left for vacation, we let the epoxy set for 7 days until we got back to ensure it cured properly.



REASSEMBLING

Once the epoxy was cured, the table was screwed back together. And it was done! This process is always something I enjoy. It allows me to try and learn new things when I am really just winging it.

FINAL DESIGN

The final design has a sleek look, and it works as a unique coffee table. These processes are messy, fast-paced, and great for learning. I think these projects always help me develop skills in building, painting, and most importantly planning. I am so excited to work on more in the future.

NEXT STEPS

Because of all of this work, I have been looking into furniture design classes at Drexel to develop this hobby more. I want to be at a point where I could just create the furniture I want to have for my house, rather than having to buy it.
